Standardised data files for systematic reviews


Your views on standardised systematic review data formats

Many thanks for agreeing to contribute to this project - your inputs are greatly appreciated!


Even in excellent systematic reviews, data are rarely reported in formats and file types that are easily understood and can be reused or repurposed for future projects, like systematic review updates or overlapping analyses. Instead, much of the review work must be repeated.


Imagine the way you backup your mobile phone and transfer your files and settings to a new handset: if systematic reviews were accompanied by a set of standardised file types describing the methods, activities and outputs, reviews could easily be updated, verified and adapted by machines.


We recognise the wasted effort that could be saved here, and we are conducting a project to establish a set of minimum standards, file types and formats that will help to ensure that systematic reviews are as transparent and interoperable as possible (meaning that their data can be easily reused and adapted for other purposes).


Our aim is to bring together systematic review methods experts from across all fields of research to propose a standard for describing the data associated with a systematic review (representing their design and results) in structured and computable ways.


Here are some important definitions/explanations:


  1. Data - here, we mean ALL information that describes a systematic review, in terms of its scope, methods and results. This includes descriptive information ('meta-data') about a systematic review's key elements (e.g. PICO) and methodology (e.g. databases searched), information and data regarding the evidence found (e.g. PRISMA flow diagram data, critical appraisal judgements), and descriptive information and study findings extracted from all studies.
  2. Disciplinary relevance - this project is aimed at establishing ideal standards across disciplines that can be applied in any sector. There is therefore a need to remain general whilst still being as practical as possible. We are doing this to make it easier to adopt across research because we feel strongly that systematic review methods are adaptable frameworks that lend themselves well to any research discipline.
  3. Ideal standards - we are not aiming to immediately bring in compulsory reporting of every detail. What we are aiming for is a standard that we can work towards.
  4. Format - working towards this standard is working towards a goal that we believe strongly will help improve the rigour, efficiency, adoption and impact of evidence synthesis: full digitisation of all data linked to a systematic review. We believe that by providing all details about a review (in terms of what what done, what was found, and what were the findings) will reduce research waste and increase efficiency and responsiveness of evidence synthesis by sharing of effort across related or overlapping reviews, and facilitating updating and upgrading of syntheses.
  5. Guiding principles - this work is guided by the principles of FAIR (Findable, Accessible, interoperable, Reusable) data and Open Science (Open Synthesis as it applies to evidence synthesis).


With these things in mind, our questions for this project are:


  • WHAT data should authors make public alongside their review manuscripts?
  • HOW should these data be provided (in what format)?


This form has space for up to 5 pairs of responses (WHAT and HOW), but if you want to provide more, please come back and submit again.
